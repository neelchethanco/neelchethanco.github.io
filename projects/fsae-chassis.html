Overview

Designed, sourced materials for, and physically fabricated a Formula SAE steel chassis. The project spanned the full engineering cycle from CAD modeling through procurement to hands-on welded assembly, resulting in a complete structural frame built to FSAE constraints.

Design Phase

Modeled the full chassis in Autodesk Inventor using parametric 3D CAD. Laid out the space frame geometry with attention to triangulation for structural efficiency and designed within Formula SAE dimensional and tubing constraints, including minimum tube sizing, required triangulation, and roll hoop geometry. The design went through 11 iterations before committing to fabrication.

Procurement

Identified all required steel tube stock and supporting materials from the CAD model. Developed a full bill of materials and sourced all components, managing the entire procurement process independently.

Fabrication and Assembly

Cut and fit steel tubing to geometry. Set up fixturing and jigging to hold frame geometry during welding. MIG welded all joints, managing heat input and distortion throughout assembly. Produced a complete, structurally assembled chassis.

Skills Demonstrated

CAD and Structural Design
Parametric 3D modeling in Autodesk Inventor, space frame layout and triangulation logic, designing to regulatory constraints, and design iteration prior to fabrication.

Engineering Judgment
Material selection for steel tube stock, member sizing for stiffness and weight trade-offs, and understanding load distribution through triangulated structures.

Fabrication
MIG welding, tube cutting and fitment, fixturing for geometric accuracy, and heat distortion management, a non-trivial challenge in welded tubular structures.

Project Execution
Bill of materials development, independent procurement, and end-to-end delivery from design file to physical artifact.

Relevance to Aerospace Engineering

Structural analysis is a core component of modeling and simulation work in aerospace and defense, including FEA of airframes, vehicle structures, and mechanical assemblies. Having physically designed and built a welded space frame provides grounded physical intuition about how structures behave under load, where stress concentrations form at weld joints, and how real fabricated geometry deviates from idealized CAD.

That intuition directly informs higher-quality simulation work: understanding what you are modeling, where the model may diverge from physical reality, and what failure modes are plausible. It also demonstrates the ability to close the loop between design and physical reality, the same validation mindset that underlies verification and validation work in modeling and simulation.
